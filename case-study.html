Popping Bubbles — Case Study

Designing an AI‑assisted browser game that responds to facial expressions. Built with p5.js, p5play, and a privacy‑conscious telemetry layer.

Overview

Product summary: Popping Bubbles is a fast, pick‑up‑and‑play browser game in three modes—Emotion, Focus, and Zen. The twist: in Emotion Mode, the game reads your facial expression (happy/sad/angry/stressed/neutral) using on‑device camera vision to influence difficulty in real time. The game is built to be lightweight, accessible, and joyful, with no installs and a mobile‑friendly layout.

My roles: Product strategy, UX research, UI design, Prototyping, Gameplay programming, Front‑end engineering, and System analytics.

Problem & inspiration: In usability sessions from previou projects, I noticed many non‑gamers bounced off traditional controls. I wanted to explore emotion as input—could the player’s expressions shape the game, so it feels more welcoming and more personal? This led to a design question: How might we make a short web game that adapts to a player’s emotion without feeling invasive?

Problem Framing

Research Approach

I combined interviews (12 participants), quick observational tests (18 people over three sessions), and a short survey (n=32) shared with classmates and friends to validate the problem. Interviews explored comfort with camera‑based interactions, what “fun” means in 5–minute games, and accessibility hurdles (e.g., keyboard dexterity). Observational tests measured drop‑off during onboarding and the first 60 seconds of play. The survey ranked the perceived fairness and enjoyability of difficulty systems.

  • Who I interviewed: 8 casual players, 2 self‑described “non‑gamers,” and 2 hobbyist devs. Mix of laptop and smart phone users; half said they avoid camera permissions by default.
  • Methods: 30‑minute moderated sessions on Zoom or in person; I recorded notes, not faces. For observational tests I used think‑aloud and time‑to‑first‑bubble metrics.
Primary research insights
  1. Friction kills fun: If the game asks for permissions or long instructions up front, people bail.
  2. Fairness matters: People enjoy games that respond to them but dislike “punishment” when they look away.
  3. Visual feedback beats text: Players learn faster from animated cues than from tooltips.
  4. Short sessions win: 2–3 minute loops with quick restarts fit smart phone habits and study breaks.
  5. Privacy is table stakes: Participants wanted camera processing on device and a clear opt‑in.
“I’m fine using my camera if it’s clearly optional and I can see what the game is reading.” — Interviewee A
“Don’t punish me for blinking or laughing. Make it rewarding.” — Interviewee C
Secondary research learnings
  • On‑device face analysis via lightweight models (e.g., TinyFace library from face-api.js) can run faster in modern browsers; this supports low‑latency emotion input while trading off a bit accuracy compare to other resource-intensive models.
  • Perceived control correlates with enjoyment in casual games; adaptive difficulty should bias toward assistance rather than punishment.
User Archetypes
The Snack Gamer

“I have 3 minutes before class; entertain me.”

  • Plays on smart phone
  • Low patience for onboarding
  • Wants quick reward loops
The Curious Non‑Gamer

“I’ll try if controls are simple.”

  • Camera hesitant; prefers opt‑in
  • Needs friendly defaults
  • Loves visual guidance
Constraints
  • Time: 6 weeks from kickoff to showcase; weekly milestones.
  • Funding: low budget; must explore free or lower cost tech.
  • Devices: must run on mid‑range laptops and smart phones (no GPU assumption).

Solution

Elevator pitch: A joyful bubble‑popping game that adapts to your Emotion. Smile to spawn bonus bubbles; a neutral face keeps standard pace; a surprised face slows the timer to give you a breather. All detection runs locally in the browser.

First development test – non-working prototype
  • Tool expoloration: I started to test cocos2d tool https://www.cocos.com/en/cocos2d-x which requires a few dependencies and Cocos Creator https://docs.cocos.com/creator/3.8/manual/en/getting-started/first-game-2d/ . After more testing and developing in this cocos2d tool, found it is not suitable to my individual project, such as outdated python version and module, limitation on game development and compilation and testing challenges.
  • Quick test: After more research, I found GDevelop 5 https://editor.gdevelop.io/ may be the next best alternative tool. I built my first development test in early July 2025 and observed the following pros and cons:
    • Pros:
      • Fast prototype
      • Free
      • No code
      • Many templates and can adjust quickly
      • Can publish easily to cloud
    • Cons:
      • Limited assets for visual
      • Limited options to adjust game settings
      • Limited features on “free” version
First working prototype

started with a barebones p5.js scene: a spawner for bubbles, a tap/click pop mechanic, and a simple timer. For emotion input I stubbed a keyboard toggle to simulate emotion to validate pacing without needing the camera yet. Then add the face-api.js to build the emotion detection system with auto-adjusting game mechanics to enhance the emotion mode.

Testing
  • Who: 10 testers across two sessions (4 on laptop, 6 on smart phone).
  • Methods: A/B modes comparing static vs. adaptive difficulty; task was “without giving any instruction, play as you desired” I logged time‑to‑goal, retries, and self‑reported fun (1–5).
Testing insights
  1. Adaptive builds increased retries and session length by ~22% without frustration.
  2. People needed a clear opt‑in for camera; a friendly overlay with live landmarks built trust.
  3. “Bonus bubbles on smile” felt better than “penalties on frown.”
  4. Animated onboarding outperformed text instructions.
Key product decisions (before → after)

Technology

  • Engine: p5.js + p5play for sprites and physics.
  • Emotion detection: light‑weight facial expression model (face-api.js) running in local library on device; no images leave the browser.
  • Data: A Cloudflare Worker writes anonymized session stats to Google Sheets for secure research study data handling. Player identity is not stored.

Tech demo: Live Demo — the repo documents build steps and model choices.

Service Blueprint & Storyboard

Service Blueprint (simplified)
  • Frontstage: Landing → camera opt‑in → animated onboarding → play loop → score/leaderboard.
  • Backstage: Model load → expression inference → difficulty rules → telemetry (Sheet append).
  • Support: CDN hosting, Cloudflare Worker, Google Sheets quota monitor.
Storyboard (key beats)
  1. Curious Non‑Gamer opens link from a friend.
  2. Sees playful opt‑in with live dots; taps “Try Emotion Mode.”
  3. Smiles; bonus bubbles burst with a sparkle sound.
  4. Shares score; replays in Zen while on the bus.

User journey testing highlighted the importance of progressive disclosure: we delay camera requests until after first interaction, and we show exactly what the model sees via landmarks. This reduced opt‑out rates during tests.

Reflection

What I'd do next
  • Ship accessibility options (camera‑free assist mode; high‑contrast UI).
  • Add a daily Focus seed and leaderboard with anonymous IDs.
  • Experiment with on‑device audio‑reactive effects for music syncing.
What failed & learnings
  • Early punishment‑heavy difficulty caused frustration → pivot to reward‑only Emotion perks.
  • Missing onboarding underperformed → replaced with animated cues.
  • Initial camera ask on page load spiked bounce and high delays → moved to post‑tap moment with faster game performance.

Overall, this project let me wear multiple hats—researcher, designer, engineer. That cross‑training made trade‑offs visible: knowing the model’s performance limits shaped the interaction design; running live playtests informed the code architecture. It’s the “Beyoncé of design” effect: singing (research), dancing (interaction), and songwriting (engineering) in harmony.
